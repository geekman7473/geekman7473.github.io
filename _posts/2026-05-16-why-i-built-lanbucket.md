LAN parties have always been a big part of my life. For the last 15 years or so I've hosted a LAN party at least once a year, if not twice a year. Over the years, obviously, a lot of things have changed in the tech industry as computers have gotten faster and what commodity hardware looks like has improved substantially. I remember distinctly that when we were first throwing these things in my friend's dingy basement, the largest problem was the underpowered hardware in commodity laptops at the time. We had to work really hard to find games that everyone's computers could even manage to load, let alone actually play smoothly. This period of time gave me a huge appreciation for older classic Win32 games. I'm talking about Command and Conquer: Red Alert 2, the Civilization series, the Age of Empires series, etc. These games, I feel, fundamentally respected players more than modern live service games. All these years later, this type of game is still what we play at the LAN parties I host.


Now, the big problem that we have perennially is file distribution. Over the last decade I've tried every solution under the sun to try to ameliorate this problem. They all have drawbacks, they're all imperfect, and I've never found a solution that works perfectly for my use case. I've heard many suggestions over the years and all of them had at least one problem that I found blocking. Many of you reading this are probably thinking of solutions for how to distribute a file to a handful of players at a LAN party right now, but I assure you that if you thought of it in the last 30 seconds, I have thought of it at some point over the last 10 years. A frustration of mine is that in this brave new world of cloud computing, the most accepted and most frictionless way of transmitting a file between two computers, even if they're sitting right next to each other connected over LAN, has become uploading that file to some data center halfway across the country and then having your buddy download it back from that same data center using some cloud storage provider like OneDrive or Google Drive or Dropbox. I understand why these solutions have proliferated, for the simple ease of use and the "it does what it says on the tin" promise, but I find it intellectually dissatisfying that for a byte to travel six feet, it must first travel a thousand miles.

## Solutions I've tried

The simplest thing I've tried to distribute a file to all of my players before they arrive at the LAN party is to tell them to download it ahead of time. This seems like the simplest, easiest way to do it. In fact, in some sense, it's the most efficient. You upload the file once, you distribute a link to some cloud storage provider (the same ones I was complaining about last paragraph), and then your users download the file and perhaps install the game before they arrive. As simple as this may sound, in practice I have discovered that almost no one will do this. Your players are already promising you a lot just to show up to your LAN party. The ones I threw when I was in high school were 24 hour events. Now that I am nearly 30, 12 hours is a more reasonable figure. Even still, asking for 12 hours of someone's time, even if it's to do something fun, is a big request, so it's not super surprising that people don't want to think too hard about doing homework before they arrive. In fact, it's bad enough that I'm also asking my players to disassemble their desktop PCs (for those of them who do not have gaming laptops) to show up to my house. So in practice, this is not a workable solution, at least not for the friends I have. If your friends are more conscientious, perhaps this will work for you.

The next, perhaps most obvious solution is to pass around a flash drive. In fact, if you have the cash on hand, just 16 flash drives. For the record, my parties are about that scale. The largest I've ever held was 35 players, and the smallest I'll do is something like eight. So you could, in practice, buy say 16 flash drives, copy onto them the games you want to play as zipped archives, hand out the flash drives when everyone shows up, and everyone copies the files off. While this does work, I find it very inelegant and rather annoying. You suddenly have this problem of needing to spend a bunch of time serially copying data onto these flash drives before the LAN party. And you also don't have a lot of recourse if you make a mistake and copy the wrong file. This also is missing a certain social element that we'll come back to later. In other words, if someone at the LAN party shows up with something that they think everyone else ought to have, even if it's just a meme or something, it's kind of a pain for them to flash that onto a flash drive and then run it around sneaker-net style to everyone at the party. It is much more convenient if there's a way for that guy to share it with everyone without needing to do this manual physical step. There are other logistical concerns too with the flash drive idea, up to and including needing to manage these things in inventory, so to speak. If I buy 16 high-speed flash drives, I don't want to lose them every single time I throw a party. If these things are going to be effectively disposable, they need to be really cheap. But really cheap flash drives are really slow and are a huge pain.

Another idea that gets proposed a lot is using, again, cloud storage to distribute these files, and then get the link to everyone at the party and they'll just download it while you're sitting there. Now, in truth, internet speeds have picked up quite a bit since I was in high school,gigabit internet service is now quite common. The problem is, if you're transferring a 10 gigabyte file and you've got 16 players sitting at the table, suddenly you're actually downloading 160 gigabytes over the internet even though there's really only 10 real gigabytes of payload there. You're just duplicating the work a bunch of times. This also means that you're splitting your gigabit connection 16 ways, so everyone's only actually getting 1000 / 16 megabits per second. For smaller files, maybe this is acceptable, but again, these games sometimes get big, so this performance problem becomes substantial. There's also a bootstrapping problem with this approach: how do I get the link to all of my players? You could use something like Discord or Facebook Messenger to send the link to everyone, but that also assumes that everyone is already connected on some social platform already. Ultimately, party planning is a social problem, not a technology problem, and convincing a group of highly opinionated nerds to use a particular platform ahead of time, especially a social platform that may or may not have data privacy concerns, is difficult and perhaps even intractable. So whatever solution we come up with needs to be zero configuration and require no accounts, no setup, no personally identifiable information, etc.

So a solution that I've tried a couple times is, why don't you just host a little local HTTP server on your network? There are a bunch of really easy ways to do this. Python has a simple HTTP server that you can just launch from the command line, and there are a number of other similar, portable, easy-to-run web server type products. This is pretty good. Zero configuration on the part of all of your guests. But it has a perhaps silly problem: you now have to tell all of your guests to type an IP address into their web browser. Now, you might think that the to the population who would go to a LAN party this would be natural, but what I've observed in practice is this ends up being a huge pain point. You end up haveing to literally go around to people's computers and type the IP address into the address bar for them because they're not capable of figuring out how to do this on their own. Your mileage may vary on this particular aspect, but this is just what I have observed over the years. Once everyone has navigated to your webserver there is still the same bottleneck. You have one computer who has these files, and you're distributing its bandwidth N ways for all of your guests. The other problem is this doesn't have a good social sharing story. These simple HTTP servers that exist are largely static and there isn't really an easy way for other users to upload files for you to share with everyone else. The configuration burden is still on the party host.

In fairness, there is an addendum to this that I hadn't considered until a friend brought it up to me while I was already working on LANBucket: what if, instead of typing an IP address, you registered a DNS entry that just pointed to your local IP address? I didn't know that this was possible. It never occurred to me to do it, but sure enough, this does actually work. 

In a similar vein, a solution I've tried before is using file shares on Windows. This can work, however it has some of the same problems as the web server idea. In theory you should be able to set up a file share that allows reads and writes, which should solve the social element. However, in practice, Windows has banned the use of passwordless (authenticationless) file shares in recent versions of Windows 11 for security reasons. I actually agree with Microsoft here. I think that in general passwordless, authenticationless file shares are a really bad idea. With that said, it was a really convenient way to solve this problem back in the day.  You could work around this by either setting some sort of password (which I think should work) or by sharing the share folder with everyone's Microsoft accounts, explicitly adding each one to the permission list. While this would work really well in an enterprise environment, in your sort of home scenario, not only does this violate our zero configuration principle, it also is a huge pain in the ass and no one wants to do that. And you end up fighting the settings that everyone has set on their individual machines for their Windows firewall settings and whatnot.

It also had the problem that you need to communicate to your guests either the IP address or the DNS name of your host machine. These DNS issues are something I should touch on more here, because you might think, "oh, I can set my machine name to something memorable and tell everyone to navigate to \\justin-machine in File Explorer." In practice this does not work for a lot of users, because they will have intentionally overridden their DNS configuration. Instead of pointing to your local router's DNS, they're pointing to Cloudflare or Google's DNS servers (1.1.1.1 or 8.8.8.8).

Another solution is to just provide computers for all of your guests and preloaded everything. If you've heard of the Austin [LAN party house](https://lanparty.house/), you'll note that this type of thing is possible. Unfortunately, I don't have the time or money or approval from my wife to set up an entire floor of my house dedicated to LAN parties. So this is a non-starter for me, and also totally violates our zero configuration principles. Though if you have a setup like that, you probably don't need a solution like LANBucket anyway.

## Enter LANBucket

This is where LANBucket comes in. LANBucket works by using multicast traffic and a number of fallback techniques to discover all the other users running LANBucket on your local network. By installing LANBucket from the Windows Store, we also get automatic updates limiting the case where a user forgot to update causing compat issues. LANBucket uses distributed file transfers based on the BitTorrent protocol. The idea being that when you're transferring a large file to a bunch of users, as soon as those users start getting a little piece of the file on their machine, they can immediately start swarming and sharing that data with the other peers who are attempting to download it at the same time. In my testing, this can provide substantial performance wins over serialized transfers like in the web server model. LANBucket also provides a very simple user experience. The idea was to model the UX after a shared folder in one of the cloud storage providers.. The view on the right, in the main page of LANBucket, shows all of the content that everyone has shared with the network. Users can download files with a single click, or they can share files by clicking and dragging them in. This, I think, provides a very simple and understandable user experience, and models the exact interactions that I'm trying to have. The idea is to facilitate this interaction: some guy at the party turns and says, "hey, I've got this really cool thing you should all look at," and clicks and drags it into LANBucket, and then everyone can just click download right away. A couple of moments later, everyone has it downloaded. No muss, no fuss.

There are a number of alternative solutions to this problem that I won't go into too much detail about here, including things like using SFTP or SSH to communicate between the computers sitting in front of you. I'll say that these are not tractable if most of your guests aren't even comfortable typing in IP addresses. Not to mention the fact that almost all my guests are Windows users. I myself am a Windows guy. I always have been. But with that said, I do want to add Linux support to LANBucket at some point in the future. So anything that requires the command line is not a workable option. There are other no-configuration file sharing tools that already exist. There's a handful that do the same sort of local network, zero configuration peer discovery that LANBucket provides. And there's even D-LAN, which does the peer discovery and does distributed transfers, which LANBucket also provides. I don't mean any disrespect to the developers of D-LAN. However, I did find their UI to be somewhat complex and not a good fit for my non-technical users. Its UX abstraction is, I find, a little bit verbose, and doesn't provide the sort of low-friction golden path that I want for LANBucket. D-LAN is a really cool tool. I'd recommend checking it out. Perhaps it would be a better fit for you than LANBucket.

## Results

I've successfully deployed LANBucket at two LAN parties now to beta test it. In both cases what my users and party guests were excited about was just the simplicity. It let us spend more time dealing with setting up the games we were playing, and less time dealing with transferring files back and forth and making sure everyone had everything they needed. I even observed users doing things like sending little batch scripts to each other over the network, or sending memes to each other, that type of thing. I've spent about a year working on LANBucket, including running many automated tests, so I would judge it to be fairly stable at this point. Since LANBucket is fundamentally based on BitTorrent, you may have questions about security. What I'll say is that LANBucket simplifies the BitTorrent protocol in a number of ways to improve performance and throughput, but as a consequence it removes some features. For example, LANBucket cannot be used over the open internet; it can only be used on your local network. Now, the other thing is, LANBucket does not use any form of encryption. I believe that there is not a security boundary between yourself and your LAN party guests. If someone wanted to install malware on my computer or the computers of the other party guests, they wouldn't need to man-in-the-middle LANBucket. Instead, they could just wrap up a virus and share it on LANBucket by calling it game.exe. To that end, I only suggest using LANBucket amongst trusted participants, like your personal friends at a LAN party in your house, that type of thing. I also wouldn't recommend using LANBucket at large public LAN parties for just the same reason, because I wouldn't recommend downloading an exe that some stranger suggests you run. I understand that there are ways to verify the digital signatures of executables that you download from an untrusted party, but I think that for a non-technical audience they should exercise caution using this tool with strangers.

I hope that you find time to try out LANBucket, and I hope that it is useful for you at your LAN parties, or just for sharing files around the house if you have, like me, a dozen or so computers in every room. You can find LANBucket at lanbucket.com or in the Microsoft Store.
